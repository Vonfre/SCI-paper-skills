# DRK1 Supports ABA-Induced Stomatal Closure in Arabidopsis Guard Cells

Lin Chen1, Mei Zhang1, Arun Patel2, and Hua Li1*

1. Department of Plant Biology, Example University, Shanghai, China
2. Center for Plant Stress Biology, Example Institute, Singapore

*Correspondence: hua.li@example.edu

## Abstract

Abscisic acid (ABA) promotes stomatal closure during water stress, but the regulatory kinases that tune the guard-cell response remain incompletely defined. Here, we characterize DRK1, a candidate Arabidopsis kinase identified from a guard-cell ABA-response screen. Loss of DRK1 did not alter basal stomatal aperture but strongly attenuated ABA-induced closure. After 10 uM ABA treatment for 2 h, wild-type stomata narrowed from 4.82 +/- 0.13 um to 2.08 +/- 0.09 um, whereas drk1-1 stomata remained wider at 3.61 +/- 0.12 um. Native-promoter complementation partially restored ABA responsiveness, supporting a DRK1-linked contribution to the phenotype. DRK1 disruption also reduced ABA-induced guard-cell ROS accumulation and attenuated induction of selected ABA-responsive marker transcripts. These results indicate that DRK1 is required for the full guard-cell response to ABA under the tested conditions. Because substrate, epistasis, and biochemical data are not yet available, we interpret DRK1 as a contributor to ABA responsiveness rather than as a direct upstream regulator of ROS production.

## Significance Statement

This study identifies DRK1 as a positive contributor to ABA-induced stomatal closure in Arabidopsis guard cells. The work links a stomatal phenotype with complementation, ROS imaging, and ABA-responsive marker expression while maintaining a bounded interpretation of mechanism.

## Introduction

Stomata regulate the exchange of carbon dioxide and water vapor between plants and the atmosphere. During water limitation, the phytohormone abscisic acid (ABA) promotes stomatal closure, helping plants restrict transpirational water loss. Guard-cell ABA responses involve coordinated changes in ion-channel activity, cytosolic calcium, protein phosphorylation, reactive oxygen species (ROS), and transcriptional regulation [1-4].

The core ABA signaling pathway includes PYR/PYL/RCAR receptors, clade A PP2C phosphatases, and SnRK2 protein kinases [5-7]. In guard cells, ROS generated by NADPH oxidases contributes to ABA signal amplification and calcium-channel activation [1,3]. Although several central components have been identified, additional kinases may shape the strength, timing, or cell-type specificity of stomatal closure. Distinguishing between a kinase that contributes to the ABA response and a kinase that directly controls a defined pathway step requires careful claim control and multiple layers of evidence.

DRK1 was selected as a candidate regulator because preliminary guard-cell expression data and mutant screening suggested altered ABA responsiveness. However, the extent to which DRK1 contributes to stomatal closure and ABA-associated cellular outputs remained unknown. We therefore combined stomatal aperture assays, genetic complementation, ROS imaging, and marker-gene expression analysis to test the role of DRK1 in the guard-cell ABA response.

Our results show that DRK1 disruption reduces ABA-induced stomatal closure, that native-promoter DRK1 complementation partially restores the response, and that DRK1 affects ABA-induced ROS accumulation and selected ABA-responsive transcripts. Together, these data support a role for DRK1 in the full guard-cell response to ABA while leaving direct pathway placement unresolved.

## Results

### DRK1 disruption attenuates ABA-induced stomatal closure

To test whether DRK1 contributes to ABA-induced stomatal closure, we compared stomatal aperture in wild-type Col-0 and drk1-1 plants after mock or ABA treatment. Under mock conditions, stomatal apertures were similar between wild type and drk1-1, with mean apertures of 4.82 +/- 0.13 um and 4.91 +/- 0.14 um, respectively (Fig. 1A and 1B). After treatment with 10 uM ABA for 2 h, wild-type stomata narrowed to 2.08 +/- 0.09 um, whereas drk1-1 stomata remained significantly wider at 3.61 +/- 0.12 um (Fig. 1B). The genotype-by-treatment interaction was significant by two-way ANOVA (P < 0.0001), indicating that DRK1 disruption selectively affected the ABA response rather than basal aperture.

The distribution of aperture values further supported this conclusion. Mock-treated wild-type and drk1-1 samples largely overlapped, whereas ABA-treated drk1-1 stomata showed a right-shifted distribution relative to ABA-treated wild type (Fig. 1C). Across three independent experiments, the ABA-induced closure index was reduced by 58% in drk1-1 compared with wild type (Fig. 1D). These results show that DRK1 is required for the full ABA-induced closure response under the tested conditions.

### Native-promoter DRK1 complementation partially restores ABA responsiveness

To determine whether the closure phenotype was linked to DRK1 disruption, we generated a complementation line expressing DRK1 under its native promoter in the drk1-1 background. Quantitative RT-PCR detected DRK1 transcript abundance in the complementation line at 0.87 +/- 0.08-fold of the wild-type level (Fig. 2A). Under mock conditions, the complementation line showed aperture values comparable to wild type and drk1-1. After ABA treatment, stomatal aperture in the complementation line decreased to 2.46 +/- 0.10 um, which was significantly narrower than drk1-1 and moderately wider than wild type (Fig. 2B).

This partial rescue supports a DRK1-linked contribution to ABA-induced closure. Because the complemented plants did not fully match wild-type aperture after ABA treatment, the data do not establish that DRK1 alone is sufficient to restore every output of the pathway. Nevertheless, recovery of most of the closure response strengthens the specificity of the drk1-1 phenotype and supports continued analysis of DRK1-associated ABA responses.

### DRK1 disruption reduces ABA-induced ROS accumulation in guard cells

ABA-induced ROS production is a well-established feature of guard-cell ABA signaling [1,3]. We therefore measured guard-cell ROS accumulation using H2DCF-DA fluorescence after mock or ABA treatment. In wild-type guard cells, ABA increased normalized ROS fluorescence 2.68 +/- 0.18-fold relative to mock (Fig. 3A and 3B). In drk1-1, ABA increased fluorescence only 1.42 +/- 0.11-fold. The complementation line showed an intermediate but substantially restored response of 2.21 +/- 0.15-fold (Fig. 3B).

These results indicate that DRK1 disruption is associated with reduced ABA-induced ROS accumulation. However, this assay does not establish whether DRK1 acts upstream of ROS production, downstream of ROS, or in a parallel branch that affects both closure and the ROS readout. We therefore interpret the ROS phenotype as pathway-associated support for the stomatal phenotype rather than as direct evidence of pathway order.

### DRK1 affects selected ABA-responsive marker transcripts

To ask whether DRK1 disruption also affects ABA-responsive transcriptional output, we measured expression of RD29B, RAB18, and ABI5 after 3 h of ABA treatment. In wild-type seedlings, RD29B, RAB18, and ABI5 increased 6.4 +/- 0.7-fold, 5.8 +/- 0.6-fold, and 3.1 +/- 0.4-fold, respectively (Fig. 4A). In drk1-1, the same transcripts increased 3.2 +/- 0.4-fold, 2.7 +/- 0.3-fold, and 1.8 +/- 0.2-fold. The complementation line partially restored transcript induction for all three markers (Fig. 4A).

These transcriptional data are consistent with DRK1 contributing to ABA responsiveness. Because the assay was performed in whole seedlings rather than isolated guard cells, the results should not be treated as direct evidence of guard-cell-specific transcriptional control. Instead, they provide supporting evidence that DRK1 disruption affects selected ABA-responsive outputs.

### Evidence-bounded working model

The combined data support a model in which DRK1 contributes to the full ABA response in guard cells (Fig. 5). In this model, DRK1 disruption weakens ABA-induced closure, reduces ABA-associated ROS accumulation, and attenuates selected ABA-responsive transcripts. The model remains bounded: DRK1 is not assigned to a specific molecular step until pathway-ordering, substrate, or biochemical evidence is available.

## Discussion

This study identifies DRK1 as a positive contributor to ABA-induced stomatal closure. The primary evidence is the impaired closure response of drk1-1 under ABA treatment, supported by partial genetic complementation. The ROS and marker-gene assays extend the phenotype to ABA-associated cellular outputs, providing a coherent but still bounded story for DRK1 function in the guard-cell ABA response.

The strongest conclusion supported by the data is that DRK1 is required for the full ABA-induced stomatal closure response under the tested conditions. This wording is intentionally narrower than a direct mechanism claim. ABA guard-cell signaling involves receptor-mediated phosphatase inhibition, kinase activation, ROS production, calcium signaling, and ion-channel regulation [1-7]. A mutant phenotype plus reduced ROS accumulation can place DRK1 within the broader ABA-response context, but it cannot prove that DRK1 directly regulates ROS production or a known ABA signaling component.

The complementation experiment strengthens the genetic interpretation of the phenotype. Native-promoter DRK1 expression restored much of the ABA closure response and partially restored ROS accumulation. Partial rescue can reflect transgene position effects, incomplete spatial or temporal expression, genetic background, or additional pathway complexity. Future work should test independent complementation lines, kinase-dead rescue constructs, and DRK1 protein abundance to determine whether kinase activity is required for the response.

The ROS results provide a useful path for mechanistic follow-up. DRK1 may act upstream of ROS production, downstream of ROS, or in a parallel branch that affects the ability of guard cells to close in response to ABA. Epistasis with NADPH oxidase mutants, pharmacological ROS rescue, time-resolved ROS imaging, and in vitro substrate assays would help distinguish these possibilities. Similarly, guard-cell-specific transcript analysis would clarify whether the marker-gene effects observed in seedlings reflect guard-cell regulation or broader ABA responsiveness.

In summary, DRK1 supports ABA-induced stomatal closure in Arabidopsis guard cells and is associated with ABA-induced ROS and transcript responses. The current evidence establishes DRK1 as a contributor to the full ABA response while defining the experiments needed to place DRK1 within the molecular pathway.

## Materials and Methods

### Plant material and growth conditions

Arabidopsis thaliana Col-0 was used as the wild-type background. The drk1-1 line was treated as a loss-of-function mutant. Seeds were surface sterilized, stratified at 4 C for 3 d, and grown under a 16 h light and 8 h dark photoperiod at 22 C. Plants used for stomatal assays were grown on soil for 4 weeks under matched humidity and light conditions.

### Complementation line

A genomic DRK1 fragment containing a 2.1 kb upstream promoter region and the full coding sequence was cloned into a binary vector and transformed into drk1-1 plants. Homozygous T3 lines were screened by antibiotic selection and PCR genotyping. A representative line with DRK1 transcript abundance near the wild-type level was used for the experiments shown here.

### Stomatal aperture assay

Fully expanded rosette leaves were floated in opening buffer for 2 h under light and then treated with mock solution or 10 uM ABA for 2 h. Epidermal peels were imaged by bright-field microscopy. Stomatal aperture was measured as pore width using ImageJ. Each biological replicate included 60 stomata per genotype and treatment, and three independent biological replicates were analyzed.

### ROS imaging

Guard-cell ROS accumulation was measured using H2DCF-DA fluorescence. Epidermal peels were incubated with 10 uM H2DCF-DA for 20 min, washed, and treated with mock solution or 10 uM ABA for 20 min. Fluorescence was imaged using identical acquisition settings across genotypes. Mean guard-cell fluorescence was normalized to the mock-treated wild-type mean within each biological replicate.

### Quantitative RT-PCR

Ten-day-old seedlings were treated with mock solution or 10 uM ABA for 3 h. Total RNA was extracted, DNase treated, and reverse transcribed. Quantitative PCR was performed using primers for RD29B, RAB18, ABI5, and ACTIN2. Relative expression was calculated by the 2^-Delta Delta Ct method using ACTIN2 as the internal control.

### Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed on biological replicate means unless otherwise noted. Stomatal aperture data were analyzed by two-way ANOVA with genotype and treatment as factors, followed by Tukey's multiple-comparison test. ROS and qRT-PCR data were analyzed by one-way ANOVA within ABA-treated groups followed by Tukey's test. P values below 0.05 were considered significant. Exact values are reported in Supplementary Table 1.

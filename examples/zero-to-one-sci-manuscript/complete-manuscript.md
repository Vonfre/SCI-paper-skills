## Abstract

Abscisic acid (ABA) promotes stomatal closure during water stress, but the kinase-linked regulatory steps that tune this guard-cell response remain incompletely resolved. Here we use a synthetic Arabidopsis manuscript example to show how `SCI-paper-skills` converts rough result bullets into a complete evidence-bounded paper. In the example dataset, loss of the candidate kinase DRK1 impaired ABA-induced stomatal closure without detectably altering basal aperture. After 10 uM ABA treatment for 2 h, wild-type stomata narrowed from 4.82 +/- 0.13 um to 2.08 +/- 0.09 um, whereas drk1-1 stomata remained wider at 3.61 +/- 0.12 um. A DRK1 complementation line partially restored closure, supporting a DRK1-linked contribution to the response. DRK1 disruption also reduced ABA-induced guard-cell ROS accumulation and attenuated induction of selected ABA-responsive marker transcripts. Together, these synthetic results support the bounded conclusion that DRK1 is required for the full guard-cell response to ABA under the tested conditions. The manuscript deliberately avoids claiming direct pathway placement, because the example does not include substrate, epistasis, or rescue experiments that would establish mechanism.

## Introduction

Stomata balance carbon dioxide uptake with water loss, making guard-cell regulation a central component of plant adaptation to fluctuating environments. During drought and related osmotic stress, ABA accumulates and triggers signaling events that reduce stomatal aperture, thereby limiting transpirational water loss. ABA guard-cell signaling has been connected to ion-channel regulation, cytosolic calcium dynamics, protein kinase activity, and reactive oxygen species (ROS) production [1-4].

The current core model places ABA perception upstream of PP2C inhibition, SnRK2 kinase activation, and downstream cellular responses [4-6]. In guard cells, ROS generation also contributes to ABA response amplification and calcium-channel activation [1,3]. Although this framework is well established, individual kinases and regulatory nodes can still modulate the strength, timing, or tissue specificity of stomatal closure. For a newly identified kinase candidate, the most important writing challenge is to avoid jumping from a phenotype to a direct mechanism before the relevant pathway evidence exists.

DRK1 is used here as a fictional candidate kinase to demonstrate the full output of the `SCI-paper-skills` workflow. The synthetic user started with only rough observations: a drk1 mutant showed weaker ABA-induced closure, a complementation line partially restored the phenotype, ROS fluorescence was lower in the mutant, and several ABA-responsive transcripts changed. The workflow converted these bullets into a claim ladder, figure order, reviewer risk analysis, and finally a complete manuscript.

We therefore present a complete synthetic research article in which DRK1 supports ABA-induced stomatal closure in Arabidopsis guard cells. The paper argues only what the synthetic evidence can support: DRK1 contributes to the full ABA response under the tested conditions and is associated with ABA-induced ROS accumulation. It does not claim that DRK1 directly phosphorylates a known ABA pathway component or acts upstream of ROS production, because those conclusions would require additional mechanism experiments.

## Results

### DRK1 disruption attenuates ABA-induced stomatal closure

To test whether DRK1 contributes to ABA-induced stomatal closure, we compared stomatal aperture in wild-type Col-0 and drk1-1 plants after mock or ABA treatment. Under mock conditions, stomatal apertures were similar between wild type and drk1-1, with mean apertures of 4.82 +/- 0.13 um and 4.91 +/- 0.14 um, respectively (Fig. 1A and 1B). After treatment with 10 uM ABA for 2 h, wild-type stomata narrowed to 2.08 +/- 0.09 um, whereas drk1-1 stomata remained significantly wider at 3.61 +/- 0.12 um (Fig. 1B). The genotype-by-treatment interaction was significant by two-way ANOVA (P < 0.0001), indicating that DRK1 disruption selectively affected the ABA response rather than basal aperture.

We next examined the distribution of stomatal aperture values across independent biological replicates. The ABA-treated drk1-1 population showed a right-shifted distribution relative to ABA-treated wild type, while the mock-treated populations largely overlapped (Fig. 1C). Across three independent experiments, the ABA-induced closure index was reduced by 58% in drk1-1 compared with wild type (Fig. 1D). These results support the claim that DRK1 is required for the full ABA-induced stomatal closure response under the tested conditions.

### Complementation partially restores ABA responsiveness in drk1-1

To determine whether the closure phenotype was linked to DRK1 disruption, we generated a complementation line expressing DRK1 under its native promoter in the drk1-1 background. Quantitative RT-PCR detected DRK1 transcript abundance in the complementation line at 0.87 +/- 0.08-fold of wild-type levels (Fig. 2A). Under mock conditions, the complementation line showed aperture values comparable to wild type and drk1-1. After ABA treatment, stomatal aperture in the complementation line decreased to 2.46 +/- 0.10 um, which was significantly narrower than drk1-1 and moderately wider than wild type (Fig. 2B).

The partial nature of the rescue is important for interpretation. It supports a DRK1-linked contribution to ABA-induced closure, but it does not prove that DRK1 alone is sufficient to fully restore every aspect of guard-cell ABA signaling. Because the complementation line restored most of the closure defect while matching wild-type DRK1 transcript abundance within the synthetic measurement range, these data strengthen the specificity of the DRK1 phenotype and justify carrying DRK1 forward in the manuscript's central story.

### DRK1 disruption reduces ABA-induced ROS accumulation in guard cells

ABA-induced ROS production is a known component of guard-cell ABA responses [1,3]. We therefore measured guard-cell ROS accumulation using H2DCF-DA fluorescence after mock or ABA treatment. In wild-type guard cells, ABA increased normalized ROS fluorescence 2.68 +/- 0.18-fold relative to mock (Fig. 3A and 3B). In drk1-1, ABA increased fluorescence only 1.42 +/- 0.11-fold. The complementation line showed an intermediate but substantially restored response of 2.21 +/- 0.15-fold (Fig. 3B).

These results indicate that DRK1 disruption is associated with reduced ABA-induced ROS accumulation. However, the experiment does not establish whether DRK1 acts upstream of ROS production, downstream of ROS, or in a parallel branch that affects both closure and ROS readout. To maintain appropriate claim strength, we interpret the ROS data as pathway-associated support rather than direct pathway placement.

### DRK1 affects selected ABA-responsive marker transcripts

To ask whether DRK1 disruption also affects ABA-responsive transcriptional output, we measured the expression of three marker genes after 3 h of ABA treatment. In wild-type seedlings, the synthetic marker genes RD29B, RAB18, and ABI5 increased 6.4 +/- 0.7-fold, 5.8 +/- 0.6-fold, and 3.1 +/- 0.4-fold, respectively (Fig. 4A). In drk1-1, the same transcripts increased 3.2 +/- 0.4-fold, 2.7 +/- 0.3-fold, and 1.8 +/- 0.2-fold. The complementation line partially restored transcript induction for all three markers (Fig. 4A).

These transcriptional data are consistent with DRK1 contributing to ABA responsiveness, but they are not used as the main mechanistic proof of stomatal closure. The qRT-PCR assay was performed in whole seedlings rather than isolated guard cells, and the selected markers report broader ABA responsiveness. For that reason, the data are positioned as supporting evidence and not as evidence that DRK1 directly controls guard-cell transcription.

### Working model

The synthetic dataset supports a model in which DRK1 contributes to the full ABA response in guard cells (Fig. 5). In this model, DRK1 disruption weakens ABA-induced closure, partially reduces ABA-induced ROS accumulation, and attenuates selected ABA-responsive transcript outputs. The model remains deliberately bounded: DRK1 is not placed at a specific molecular step until additional epistasis, substrate, or biochemical experiments are available.

## Discussion

This complete synthetic manuscript demonstrates how a rough result set can be converted into a coherent SCI-style article while preserving evidence boundaries. The strongest conclusion supported by the example data is that DRK1 is required for the full ABA-induced stomatal closure response under the tested conditions. This conclusion is supported by the mutant phenotype, partial rescue by DRK1 complementation, and associated changes in ABA-induced ROS accumulation and marker-gene expression.

The most important writing decision is the restraint placed on mechanism. ABA guard-cell signaling involves receptor-mediated phosphatase inhibition, kinase activation, ROS production, calcium signaling, and ion-channel control [1-6]. A mutant phenotype plus reduced ROS accumulation can place DRK1 within the broader ABA response context, but it cannot prove direct biochemical regulation. Direct pathway placement would require experiments such as kinase-dead rescue, substrate identification, epistasis with ROS-production mutants, pharmacological rescue, time-resolved pathway assays, or guard-cell-specific complementation.

The complementation result strengthens the specificity of the phenotype. In the synthetic dataset, native-promoter DRK1 expression partially restored ABA-induced closure and ROS accumulation. Partial rescue can have several explanations, including transgene position effects, expression timing, incomplete promoter coverage, or contribution from linked genetic background. A real manuscript should therefore report independent complementation lines, protein abundance if antibodies are available, and segregation or whole-genome evidence when necessary. The example keeps the claim at "DRK1-linked contribution" rather than "DRK1 is sufficient."

The ROS and transcript data broaden the story but do not replace direct mechanism. ROS imaging connects the stomatal phenotype to a known ABA-response feature, while qRT-PCR suggests that DRK1 disruption affects selected ABA-responsive outputs. Because the transcript assay uses whole seedlings in this example, it should be discussed as systemic support rather than guard-cell-specific proof. This distinction is the kind of claim control that the `sci-result-to-claim` and `sci-citation-control` skills are intended to enforce.

In summary, the synthetic manuscript presents DRK1 as a contributor to ABA-induced stomatal closure and provides a complete example of the final output expected from the skill suite. A real project would next verify all journal requirements, replace the synthetic dataset with real data, validate references, and decide whether additional mechanism experiments are needed for the chosen target journal.

## Materials And Methods

### Plant material and growth conditions

Synthetic Arabidopsis thaliana Col-0 was used as the wild-type background. The fictional drk1-1 line was treated as a loss-of-function mutant for demonstration. Seeds were surface sterilized, stratified at 4 C for 3 d, and grown in controlled chambers under a 16 h light and 8 h dark photoperiod at 22 C. Plants used for stomatal assays were grown on soil for 4 weeks under matched humidity and light conditions.

### Complementation line

For the synthetic complementation experiment, a genomic DRK1 fragment containing a 2.1 kb upstream promoter region and the full coding sequence was cloned into a binary vector and transformed into drk1-1 plants. Homozygous T3 lines were screened by antibiotic selection and PCR genotyping. One representative line with DRK1 transcript abundance near the wild-type level was used for the example dataset.

### Stomatal aperture assay

Fully expanded rosette leaves were floated in opening buffer for 2 h under light, followed by treatment with mock solution or 10 uM ABA for 2 h. Epidermal peels were imaged with bright-field microscopy. Stomatal aperture was measured as pore width using ImageJ. Each biological replicate included 60 stomata per genotype and treatment, and three independent biological replicates were analyzed.

### ROS imaging

Guard-cell ROS accumulation was measured using H2DCF-DA fluorescence. Epidermal peels were incubated with 10 uM H2DCF-DA for 20 min, washed, and treated with mock solution or 10 uM ABA for 20 min. Fluorescence was imaged using identical acquisition settings across genotypes. Mean guard-cell fluorescence was normalized to the mock-treated wild-type mean within each biological replicate.

### Quantitative RT-PCR

For the synthetic transcript assay, 10-day-old seedlings were treated with mock solution or 10 uM ABA for 3 h. Total RNA was extracted, DNase treated, and reverse transcribed. Quantitative PCR was performed using gene-specific primers for RD29B, RAB18, ABI5, and ACTIN2. Relative expression was calculated by the 2^-Delta Delta Ct method using ACTIN2 as the internal control.

### Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed on biological replicate means unless otherwise noted. Stomatal aperture data were analyzed by two-way ANOVA with genotype and treatment as factors, followed by Tukey's multiple-comparison test. ROS and qRT-PCR data were analyzed by one-way ANOVA within the ABA-treated groups followed by Tukey's test. Synthetic P values below 0.05 were considered significant. Exact values are reported in Supplementary Table 1.
